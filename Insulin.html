Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes mellitus or commonly known as diabetes, is a metabolic disease that is characterized by high blood glucose levels in the bloodstream. It was first described in the Egyptian manuscript 1500 B.C. The first discovery related to diabetes mellitus was made by the Indian physician Sushruta and surgeon Charaka during 400-500 A.D.. These doctors identified two types of diabetes. However, Aretaeus the Cappadocian was the first one to make the first full description of the disease and named the disease. The term mellitus was given by the British general surgeon John Rollo in 1798. Mellitus comes from latin and it means “sweat like honey” and it was chosen because the urine of the patients had a strong sweet taste. He chose that term in order to distinguish it from another disease called diabetes insipidus.

Before Insulin

Before the discovery of insulin, the treatment of diabetes mellitus was based on calorie restricted diets and all patients were advised to reduce as much as they can sugar and dietary starch intake. Due to the insufficiency of the treatment, the life expectancy for children patients was very short and the prognosis for adult onset diabetes was poor.

Experiment

In 1992, an orthopedic surgeon called Frederick Banting had an idea in order to find the cure for the disease. He thought of isolating pancreatic islets extracts by ligating the pancreatic ducts of dogs and keeping them alive until the acini degenerated. In this way the islets could be isolated. Having this idea, he contacted John Macleod, who was a professor of physiology and together they started this experiment on 17th of March 1921.
These two scientists managed to conduct successful experiments by later that year and they named the pancreatic extract insletin. They found that isletin has the ability to lower glucose in the bloodstream. At the end of 1921, the biochemist J.B. Collip joined their team in order to help them purify insletin for human use. The first injection took place on 11th of January 1922. Insletin was injected into a 14 years old boy but it did not have any significant effect by the first injection. However, repeated injections showed very promising results. By the end of the experiment the blood glucose and glycosuria had decreased and ketonuria had disappeared.
In 1923, the Nobel Prize in Physiology and Medicine was awarded to Frederick Banting and John Macleod.

Evolution of Treatment

  • Eli Lilly : they achieved insulin production from animal’s pancreas.

  • August Krogh from University of Copenhagen : in 1923 brought insulin to Scandinavia.

  • Till then in order for insulin to function multiple injections were required daily.

  • H.C. Hadedorn ( a Denmank dentist) : during 1930s achieved prolongation of insulin duration of action by adding protamine.

  • Scott and Fisher : prolonged the duration of action by adding zinc.

  • Production of long acting animal insulin.

  • David Goeddel and his colleagues : in 1978 the first recombinant DNA insulin was made.

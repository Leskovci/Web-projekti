PAP SMEAR

Description

A Pap smear, also known as a Pap test, is a common and preventive procedure to test women for cervical cancer. The procedure is done by collecting cells from a woman's cervix. Pap smear is done in order to detect cervical cancer early because that gives a great chance of treating and curing the disease. Besides diagnosing cervical cancer, Pap smear is also helpful to detect changes in the cervical cells that might suggest that cancer is going to develop in the future. Gynecologists suggest starting this process by the age of 21. If results are normal then the procedure is repeated after a year. If any abnormality is found that does not suggest the presence of cancer, then gynecologists recommend more screening such as colposcopy using a special magnifying instrument (colposcope) to examine the tissues of the cervix, vagina and vulva and to repeat the process in 6 months. If cancerous cells are detected then therapy is necessary.

Some terms that are used to interpret the results are:

  • Atypical squamous cells of undetermined significance (ASCUS)

    • In this case, the Pap smear reveals slight abnormalities but it does not necessarily suggest that precancerous lesions are present. The doctor reanalyzes the sample to check for the presence of viruses known to promote the development of cancer, such as some types of human papillomavirus (HPV). If no high-risk viruses are present, the abnormal cells are not of a great concern.

  • Squamous intraepithelial lesion

    • In this case, precancerous cells may be present. If the changes are low grade, it means the size, shape and other characteristics of the cells suggest that if a precancerous lesion is present, then many years are required for cancer to develop. If the changes are high grade, then cancer may develop sooner.

  • Atypical glandular cells

    • In this case further testing is required in order to determine if it is cancerous lesions or not.

  • Squamous cell cancer or adenocarcinoma cells

    • In this case, the cells are so abnormal that most certainly is cancer.

First Approach of the Experiment

The Pap test was done by George Nicholas Papanikolaou, a Greek doctor. In 1916, he began his studies and experiments with guinea pigs. While studying sex chromosomes, he deduced that reproductive cycles in the experimental animals could be timed by examining smears of their vaginal secretions. In 1920, he began to focus his studies on the cytopathology of the human reproductive system. In 1928 he published the findings of his studies which focused on the differences between the cytology of normal and malignant cervical cells upon a simple viewing of swabs smeared under the microscope. Surprisingly, this publication went largely unnoticed.

Recognition

In 1943 he collaborated with Dr Herbert Traut, a gynaecological pathologist. Together they published their landmark book in 1943, Diagnosis of Uterine Cancer by the Vaginal Smear. In this book they described all the physiological changes that occur to the female body during menstruation and the influence of hormones and malignancy in vaginal cytology. Their findings showed that normal and abnormal smears taken from the vagina and cervix. As Pap smear was very easy to perform and its cost was very little it found widespread use and resulted in a significant decline in the incidence of cervical cancer.

HEART VALVE REPAIR

Closed Mitral Valve Repair

First Successful Surgery

In 1923, at Peter Bent Brigham Hospital the first successful heart valve surgery took place. The surgery was a mitral valve repair and it was performed by Dr. Elliot C. Cutler. The patient was a young girl who suffered from rheumatic mitral valve stenosis. Her condition was so severe that she was in a comatose state due to low cardiac output. After the success of the surgery Dr. Cutler decided to develop a device in order to alleviate mitral stenosis. However, he did not find success in this idea as its principle was to excise a large portion of the mitral valve which unfortunately led to severe mitral regurgitation and all his subsequent patients died.

Open Mitral Valve Repair

Early Stages

  • In 1957, the first mitral valve repair for mitral insufficiency took place at the University of Minnesota. The surgery was performed by Dr. C. Walton Lillehei.
  • Dr. Nina Starr Braunwald implanted the first artificial mitral valve at the National Institutes of Health. This artificial valve was a home-made device that had never been produced commercially.
  • During the 1960s, Dr. Albert Starr and his colleague M. Lowell Edwards at the University of Oregon revolutionized mitral valve surgery. The two scientists successfully developed the first commercial artificial mitral valve.
  • Dr. Miller and his colleagues at Stanford University realized and later informed about how important is the mitral apparatus for the purpose of maintaining good ventricular function after a successful mitral valve operation.

Evolution

In 1983, Dr. Alain F. Carpentier of the University of Paris published a paper called “The French Correction” in the Journal of Thoracic and Cardiovascular Surgery. In his paper he demonstrated the basic pathophysiological classification of mitral valve lesions and suggested the tools and a plan for how to successfully repair mitral valve regurgitation and especially in patients who suffered from degenerative myxomatous mitral valve disease. His paper received such recognition that many surgeons around the world got inspired by it and decided to perform mitral valve repair operations. The rate of successful mitral valve repair surgery was increasing drastically and this resulted in earlier referral of patients with mitral valve disease for surgical repair. What made these operations so successful was that they depended on the concept of the interactive service line, a collaborative network of cardiology, cardiac surgery and cardiac anesthesia working together.
